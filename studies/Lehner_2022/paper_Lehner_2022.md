Idiopathic generalized epilepsies are a group of sleep related epilepsy syndromes with sleep deprivation as a strong trigger for seizures and increased spike-wave activity during sleep and transition to sleep. Neuropsychological de /uniFB01 cits are common in Idiopathic generalized epilepsy patients. Learning and memory processes are closely linked to sleep. Therefore, this systematic review and meta-analysis investigates the evidence of sleep disturbances in Idiopathic generalized epilepsy patients. A search of the databases EMBASE, Medline and Scopus identi /uniFB01 ed 22 studies comparing polysomnographic parameters and scores of sleep questionnaires between Idiopathic generalized epilepsy patients and healthy controls. Random effect univariate meta-analyses revealed reduced sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency, total sleep time, proportion of N2 stage and prolonged REM onset latency in Idiopathic generalized epilepsy patients. Selfassessed sleep quality of patients measured by the Pittsburgh sleep quality index was lower in two thirds of reporting studies. Considering the in /uniFB02 uence on behavioral issues, cognitive performance and quality of life, the revealed alteration in sleep architecture and lower subjective sleep quality emphasizes the importance of screening for sleep disturbances in the medical care of patients with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy.

## 1. Introduction

Idiopathic generalized epilepsies (IGE, recognized as a subtype of genetic generalized epilepsy) account for 15 e 20% of all epilepsies and include well-known epilepsy syndromes described by the International League Against Epilepsy (ILAE): Childhood absence epilepsy, Juvenile absence epilepsy, Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy (JME), and Epilepsy with generalized tonic-clonic seizures alone (GTCS) [1 e 3]. Common features include onset in childhood or adolescence, absence of structural brain abnormalities, and generalized spike-wave or polyspike-wave discharges with normal background on electroencephalogram (EEG). Typical semiologies encompass absence, myoclonic, and generalized tonic-clonic seizures [3,4]. In terms of etiology, many genetic variants with often complex inheritance are known, but the extent of their individual in /uniFB02 uence is not conclusively clari /uniFB01 ed and subject of ongoing research [5]. Although prognosis is good and pharmacoresponsiveness is high in children with IGE compared to structural epilepsies or those of unknown origin, the remission rate and age at remission varies across the IGE syndromes; further there is a growing body of evidence suggesting poorer psychosocial, cognitive and behavioral outcomes compared to healthy controls [3,6,7]. According to a meta-analysis of 26 case-control studies, many domains of cognition are impaired in people with IGE [8], however IGE are not associated with intellectual disability [3].

The relationship between epilepsy and sleep is complex and in /uniFB02 uenced by various factors. Seizures often occur at certain times of day depending on epilepsy type. Seizures, interictal epileptic discharges, and antiseizure medication (ASM) can lead to impaired sleep quality. Sleep fragmentation in turn lowers the seizure threshold [9,10]. Disturbed sleep can cause daytime sleepiness, behavioral issues, impaired cognitive performance, and lower quality of life [10]. Sleep is considered to impact learning and memory processes. Especially slow wave sleep and REM sleep are suggested to be involved in nocturnal memory consolidation [11]. Decreased strength of cortical synapses during sleep due to pruning processes is represented by the declining slope of slow waves, and linked to neuronal recovery and good cognitive performance [12 e 15]. Higher spindle density is indicative of a more ef /uniFB01 cient subcortical-cortical connectivity and associated with superior intellectual and learning abilities and better social behavior [16]. In epilepsy, this subcortical-cortical connectivity can be altered due to epileptic ripples impairing memory transfer from the hippocampus through the thalamus to cortical structures [17]. Cyclic alternating pattern (CAP), a sign of build-up and maintenance of deep sleep achieved through a process of instability, is another important element of sleep microstructure and often altered in epilepsy. Spike waves can be driven by conversion of slow cortical oscillation of CAP into current paroxysmal discharges [18 e 20].

In IGE, typical generalized spike-wave (GSW) activity occurs more frequently in transition to sleep and in sleep with predominance during NREM sleep, especially during sleep stages N1 and N2. Furthermore, sleep deprivation leads to increased GSW activity independently of state of vigilance [4, 21]. In absence seizures, generation of ictal generalized spike-wave discharges occurs in the wake state, but also during transitions from wakefulness to NREM sleep or from NREM sleep to REM sleep. Polyspike-wave discharges in JME are more frequently observed during transition from NREM sleep to wakefulness, particularly during morning awakening [22]. Accordingly, seizures in people with JME or GTCS typically happen in the /uniFB01 rst 2 h after awakening and are often provoked by sleep deprivation [9]. On a sleep microstructure level, CAP promotes appearance of GSW [22]. As sleep spindles and GSW originate from the same thalamocortical circuit, their interrelationship is an important object of research [4,21,22].

A recently published systematic review investigated sleep dif/uniFB01 culties and architecture in children and adolescents with epilepsy and revealed altered subjective and objective sleep parameters suggesting impaired sleep quality compared to healthy controls. As the meta-analyses included patient groups with various epilepsy syndromes, the authors proposed future research focusing on particular epilepsy types [23]. In this systematic review we investigate the evidence of impaired sleep quality and altered sleep architecture in people with IGE compared to healthy controls. Therefore, we address the following questions: 1) Is there evidence of impaired sleep quality and altered sleep architecture in people with IGE compared to healthy controls measured by polysomnographic parameters or scores of sleep questionnaires? 2) Are there differences in quality of sleep and sleep architecture between individual IGE syndromes? 3) Are there differences in quality of sleep and sleep architecture between drug-naïve and ASM treated patients?

This is, to our knowledge, the /uniFB01 rst systematic review summarizing the evidence of sleep quality and sleep architecture in Idiopathic generalized epilepsy.

## 2. Methods

## 2.1. Protocol registration

The systematic review was conducted in accordance with PRISMA guidelines [24]. The protocol was registered in the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO, registration number CRD42020222120).

## 2.2. Search strategies

To identify eligible studies, a search using keywords (Embase, Medline, Scopus), Emtree (Embase), and MeSH (Medline) terms was applied to electronic databases Embase, Medline and Scopus on November 25, 2020, updated on the November 16, 2021. A detailed search string description is provided in Appendix A. First, titles and abstracts were screened for relevant literature. Subsequently, potentially eligible studies were read in full by two reviewers (JL/JF) independently and in duplicate. In case of disagreement about study eligibility, studies were discussed with a third reviewer (AD) until a consensus was reached. Furthermore, we searched reference lists of full-text screened articles for additional records.

## 2.3. Selection criteria

We included cohort and cross-sectional studies published between 1990 and 2021 meeting the following eligibility criteria: 1) Original research published in peer-reviewed journals. 2) Study participants with IGE (as de /uniFB01 ned by ILAE criteria) are compared to a matched healthy control group. 3) Studies investigate sleep quality and architecture measured by polysomnography, overnight videoEEG, or published sleep questionnaires.

We excluded studies if 1) participants with other types of epilepsy or neurological diseases were included and no separate analysis for the IGE or healthy control group was provided; 2) no full text study was published; 3) the publication language was not English, French, German, Italian or Spanish.

## 2.4. Data items and outcomes

Sample characteristics (sample size, epilepsy syndrome, semiology, ILAE criteria year, mean age, age at diagnosis and time since

diagnosis, use of ASM, seizure frequency, nocturnal activation of epileptic discharges, applied sleep classi /uniFB01 cation system) and outcome-related items (polysomnographic parameters of sleep macrostructure and microstructure, scores of sleep questionnaires) were extracted by two reviewers (JL/JF) independently and in duplicate using a standardized form. A third reviewer (AD) was consulted for advice as needed. We used the ILAE classi /uniFB01 cation 2017 for terminology in our data extraction form, although most studies applied earlier published ILAE criteria.

As an objective measure for sleep quality and architecture, polysomnographic parameters were evaluated by meta-analyses using standardized mean differences between IGE and control group as effect sizes. Results of sleep questionnaires in the individual studies were summarized qualitatively.

## 2.5. Quality assessment

A risk of bias assessment was conducted based on the STROBE statement (Strengthening the reporting of observational studies in epidemiology) [25].

## 2.6. Sleep quality measurements

Sleep quality measurements involve subjective (e.g. sleep questionnaires) and objective (e.g. polysomnography) methods. Polysomnography (PSG) records parameters of sleep macrostructure, such as duration and onset latency of different sleep stages, total sleep time, transitions between sleep stages and interruptions with wake bouts, as originally de /uniFB01 ned by Rechtschaffen and Kales, 1968 [26]. In 2007, the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) /uniFB01 rst published modi /uniFB01 ed guidelines for sleep classi /uniFB01 cation, which have been regularly updated since [27]. Microstructural sleep elements, measured by overnight EEG, include CAP [18], sleep spindle density and structure [16], and slow wave activity and slow wave slope [14].

## 2.7. Meta-analyses

We used standardized mean difference (Cohen's d) as effect measure in order to conduct random effect univariate metaanalyses (with Der Simonian-Laird estimator for heterogeneity of variance across studies) on the following sleep macrostructure items: total sleep time (min), sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency (%), sleep onset latency (min), REM onset latency (min), total N1 (%), total N2 (%), total N3 (%), total REM (%) and arousal index (events/h). A Cohen's d of 0.2 indicates a small, 0.5 a medium, and 0.8 a large effect [28]. Results of each analysis are displayed in a forest plot. Heterogeneity between included studies is estimated by I 2 statistic. The potential impact of publication biases was investigated by funnel plots and Egger's test.

We did not calculate effect estimates for sleep parameters with fewer than three available data sets (total NREM (%), CAP time (min), CAP rate (CAP time/total NREM time), sleep spindle density and con /uniFB01 guration), or if a sleep parameter was unlikely to be normally distributed due to standard deviations resulting in negative values for events per hour (apnea-hypopnea index, periodic limb movement index).

Subgroup analyses were conducted for sleep parameters of JME patients if at least three different data sets were available. For further distinction we calculated effect estimates for sleep parameters of drug-naïve and anti-seizure-drug-treated JME patients.

## 3. Results

## 3.1. Study selection and characteristics

Twenty-two studies met selection criteria [Fig. 1). Polysomnographic parameters [29 e 43] were reported in /uniFB01 fteen and sleep questionnaires [31 e 33,35,37,38,40 e 42,44 e 50] in sixteen articles (some overlapping). Twelve studies included patients with mixed IGE syndrome diagnoses. Ten studies reported JME patients only. Detailed information about included studies and patients ' characteristics is provided in Table 1 and Appendix B.

Of the studies reporting polysomnographic parameters, eight involved participants with mixed IGE syndromes and seven included patients with only JME. Mean participant age in polysomnographic studies ranged from 9 to 25 years, whereas mean age in JME-only populations ranged from 21 to 25 years. Onset of disease and time since diagnosis ranged from 8 to 21 years (reported in 8/15 studies) and from 30 to 135 months (reported in 6/15 studies) respectively. Patients suffered from generalized tonicclonic seizures, myoclonic jerks and absences. One patient showed additional focal onset seizures [41]. One patient carried a diagnosis of epilepsy with myoclonic absences, not excluded because the diagnosis still falls on the spectrum of genetic generalized epilepsy [36]. Apart from two studies including only drugnaïve patients, most reported ASM use in all (6 studies) or some of the patients (7 studies). Valproic acid (VPA) was most commonly used (10 studies). Treatment with ethosuximide (3 studies), carbamazepine, lamotrigine, levetiracetam (2 studies each), phenytoin, clobazam, and topiramate (1 study each) was reported as well. Most patients received monotherapy.

Participants from studies reporting sleep questionnaires carried diagnoses of JME in nine and mixed IGE syndromes in seven articles. Age ranges (9 e 79 years) across individual studies were wide. Data about age at diagnosis and time since diagnosis were often missing in studies with older participants. Where ASM status (13 studies) was reported, VPA was most commonly used.

## 3.2. Meta-analyses of sleep macrostructure

Figs. 2 e 10 show forest plots from univariate metanalyses conducted on parameters of sleep macrostructure for the primary analyses. Forest plots of subgroup analyses are provided in Appendix C.

Two research groups published a total of eight out of the /uniFB01 fteen studies reporting polysomnographic parameters, so it could not be ruled out that participants overlapped between the individual studies of these two research groups (research group 1, n ¼ 3 [31,32,35]; research group 2, n ¼ 5 [33,37 e 40]). To avoid possible duplicate representation of the same population, only one study from each group was included into our meta-analyses. For the primary analyses we preferentially included sleep parameters from Hamdy et al., 2020 [32] for research group 1 and Nayak et al., 2016 [38] for research group 2. Reasons for this decision are discussed in the risk of bias section. Standard mean differences (SMD) for the remaining studies from each research group are provided below the respective forest plots. Furthermore, we present alternatively calculated overall effect sizes and estimates of heterogeneity resulting from replacement of sleep parameters by the values of each study of the two research groups separately to assess the impact of our study selection on the result of each meta-analysis.

## 3.4. Results of sleep questionnaires

The Pittsburgh sleep quality index (PSQI) was the most frequently used sleep questionnaire, utilized in 12 out of 16 studies (2/3 studies from research group 1, 6/6 studies from research group 2) [31 e 33,37,38,40 e 42,46,48 e 50]. The PSQI measures sleep quality and disturbances retrospectively over one month by selfassessment [51]. Signi /uniFB01 cantly higher scores indicating impaired sleep quality in the patient group are reported in eight articles (including 6/6 studies from research group 2) [33,37,38,40 e 42,46,49]. The other four studies showed no signi /uniFB01 -cant difference between patients and controls (including both studies from research group 1) [31,32,48,50].

Nine studies applied the Epworth sleepiness scale (ESS) (0/3 studies from research group 1, 6/6 studies from research group 2) [33,37,38,40 e 42,46,49,50]. The ESS assesses daytime sleepiness by subjectively reported probability to fall asleep in eight situations of daily life [52]. Only three studies (all from research group 2) reported signi /uniFB01 cantly more daytime sleepiness in patients compared to controls [33,38,46]. One study [47] showed signi /uniFB01 cantly higher scores in patients than controls on the Pediatric daytime sleepiness scale [53].

The Morningness-Eveningness questionnaire (MEQ) was conducted in three studies [44,45,48]. The MEQ offers a classi /uniFB01 cation into morning, intermediate and evening types by calculating a total score across 19 self-assessed items. High scores indicate morning type, low scores indicate evening type [54]. Patients and healthy controls of all three studies were predominantly classi /uniFB01 ed as intermediate type [44,45,48].

Two studies reported the Munich chronotype questionnaire (MCQ) [44,50], which explores people's chronotype by asking about usual sleeping times on workdays and non-workdays [55]. While Hofstra et al., 2010 [44] observed earlier mid-sleep and total sleep time on non-workdays, Unterberger et al., 2015 [50] noted earlier mid-sleep on workdays and generally earlier sleep onset in patients compared to controls.

Maganti et al., 2006 [47] conducted the Pediatric sleep questionnaire [56] showing signi /uniFB01 cantly more sleep-disordered breathing, excessive daytime sleepiness, and parasomnias in patients than healthy controls. Mekky et al., 2017 [35] reported signi /uniFB01 cantly more JME patients with insomnia, excessive daytime sleepiness and limb movement compared to controls by applying the Psychometric sleep assessment instruments: an Arabic version for sleep evaluation [57]. Unterberger et al., 2015 [50] showed no signi /uniFB01 cant difference in prevalence of sleep disorders between patients with IGE and controls according to the Munich parasomnia scale, Innsbruck REM sleep behavior disorder inventory and Berlin questionnaire [58 e 60]. Detailed sleep questionnaire results are provided in Appendix D.

## 3.5. Quality assessment and risk of bias

Most studies reported age and gender as matching criteria between patients and controls, but reports of patients ' characteristics were incomplete in nearly half of studies. The majority of authors described recruitment and data collection process in detail and all explained applied measurement and statistical methods. Nearly half of studies identi /uniFB01 ed predictors, confounder and effect modi /uniFB01 er at study level, whereas efforts to address potential sources of bias were missing in two thirds of articles. Results of all planned analyses were reported independently of statistical signi /uniFB01 cance in all studies with one exception [47]. Detailed quality assessment of included studies is provided in Appendix E.

Possible sources of bias on review level consisted in signi /uniFB01 cantly different total time in bed between groups in some studies in /uniFB02 uencing sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency, and usage of different sleep classi /uniFB01 cation systems. Most studies used AASM or Rechtschaffen &amp; Kales, one study reported their own classi /uniFB01 cation criteria [36], and another did not specify [42]. Again, only one study each from two research groups was included in each meta-analysis to prevent overrepresentation of possibly overlapping populations. Reasons for selecting Hamdy et al., 2020 [32] from research group 1 included recent publication date, equal population size between patient and controls, and inclusion of mixed IGE syndromes. If data were not available from this study, sleep parameters by Mekky et al., 2017 [35] were used instead. We considered Nayak et al., 2016 [38] the /uniFB01 rst option for research group 2 because macro- and microstructural sleep parameters were reported for anti-seizure-drug treated and drug-naïve patients separately. The only newer publication by this research group investigated sleep macrostructure only and was therefore not /uniFB01 rst choice [39].

## 4. Discussion

## 4.1. Summary of /uniFB01 ndings

Idiopathic generalized epilepsies are a group of sleep-related epilepsy syndromes with an increased spike-wave activity during sleep and transition to sleep, in which sleep deprivation can trigger seizures and interictal epileptic discharges [4,9,21]. This systematic review investigates the evidence of impaired sleep quality and altered sleep architecture in people with IGE compared to healthy controls.

Meta-analyses of sleep macrostructure measured by PSG indicated reduced sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency (SE) (medium-large effect), total sleep time (TST) and proportion of sleep stage N2 (%N2) (both small-medium effect) in patients compared to controls. In subgroup analyses for JME patients only, the effect for SE was even more evident. There was no signi /uniFB01 cant difference for the abovementioned parameters between anti-seizure-drug-treated and drug-naïve JME patients. Furthermore, REM onset latency was signi /uniFB01 cantly longer in IGE patients (small-medium effect). Metaanalyses of other macrostructural parameters showed either no signi /uniFB01 cant difference between groups (proportion of sleep stage N3 (%N3), sleep onset latency) or inconsistent results depending on selected study from research group 1 and 2 (proportion of sleep stage N1 and REM sleep (%REM), arousal index).

The SE is in /uniFB02 uenced by total time in bed, reported only by six included articles [29,33,34,37 e 39]. In four studies from research group 2, total time in bed was signi /uniFB01 cantly longer in patients than controls [33,37 e 39]. This could lead to underestimation of SE in IGE patients. Interestingly, Nayak et al., 2015 [37] reported signi /uniFB01 cantly lower TST for patients, even with less time spent in bed compared to controls. Against the possible assumption of increased daytime sleepiness caused by impaired sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency and shortened sleep time, only three of nine studies applying the ESS reported signi /uniFB01 -cantly higher scores for patients compared to controls [33,38,46].

Given the evidence for neuropsychological de /uniFB01 cits in IGE patients [8], consideration of sleep stages presumably involved in learning processes (%N2, %N3 and %REM [10,11]) is of interest. According to our results, patients spent signi /uniFB01 cantly less time in N2 stage, which is thought to be important for nocturnal memory consolidation because of its high density of sleep spindles [10, 11]. In turn, a systematic review investigating sleep disruption in children with various epilepsy syndromes revealed higher %N2 in patients compared to controls [23].One of our included studies discussed altered distributions of sleep spindles throughout the night with less occurrence during N2 stage in untreated people with GSW compared to anti-seizure-drug-treated IGE patients and healthy controls as a potential sign of their dif /uniFB01 culty to reach deep sleep [36]. On a meta-analysis level, we found no difference in %N3 and %REM between both groups, but REM onset latency was signi /uniFB01 cantly longer in IGE patients compared to controls. Hamdy et al., 2020 [32] postulated REM sleep duration and latency as predictors of seizure control. In their prospective cohort study, patients with inadequately controlled epilepsy despite VPA during the follow-up period showed signi /uniFB01 cantly shorter %REM and longer REM onset latency at baseline (before VPA initiation) compared to seizure-free patients and healthy controls [32]. Interestingly, polysomnographic investigations of sleep-related hypermotor epilepsy (SHE) revealed similar macrostructural differences regarding REM sleep with simultaneously increased %N3. Prolonged REM onset latency is interpreted by epileptic sleep fragmentation most prominent during NREM sleep of the /uniFB01 rst sleep cycle and increased %N3 might represent a compensation mechanism for not restorative sleep [61,62]. Another study suggested reduced %N3 in patients with drug-resistant epilepsies compared to controls, while % REM was decreased and REM latency was increased [63].

Reviewing different objective sleep examination methods, Krystal at al., 2008 [64] pointed out the lower sensitivity of traditional PSG compared to CAP to detect impaired sleep quality. Terzano et al., 1989 [65] showed GSW occurrence predominantly during phase A of CAP sequences in IGE patients. Gigli et al., 1992 [66] reported similar /uniFB01 ndings in JME patients with signi /uniFB01 cantly higher CAP rate compared to healthy controls. Similarly, patient groups from the two CAP-based studies ful /uniFB01 lling our inclusion criteria showed higher CAP rates compared to healthy controls [38,43]. The fact that, at the same time, macrostructural sleep parameters did not differ in Terzano et al., 1992 [43], supports the lacking sensitivity of traditional PSG for sleep disruption. Furthermore, CAP cycles involving epileptic discharges were prolonged compared to those without epileptic activity [43]. Apart from the above-mentioned study showing lower percentages of sleep spindles during N2 sleep in untreated people with GSW [36], no investigations of other microstructural sleep parameters were found.

Considering sleep alterations in those epilepsy syndromes associated with impaired cognitive functioning, we wonder which sleep parameters in /uniFB02 uence neuropsychological performance the most. Reduced sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency is common in various epilepsy types, suggesting sleep disruption through epileptic discharges. Similarly, increased CAP rates are described in other sleep-related epilepsy syndromes with CAP-promoted epileptic discharges, e.g. SHE [61,62]. However, relative duration of macrostructural sleep stages (particularly %N2 and %N3) vary within IGE as well as across different epilepsy syndromes. Therefore, future research could investigate CAP characteristics during the individual NREM sleep stages to help quantify the impact of sleep instability related to interictal EEG paroxysms. Moreover, investigation of slow wave slope and its potential prognostic importance for cognitive development is of special interest, since a disruption in its nocturnal

decrease was shown in patients suffering from developmental and epileptic encephalopathy with continuous spike and wave activated in sleep and infantile epileptic spasm syndrome [14,15,67]. Since altered slow wave slope was described primarily in developmental and epileptic encephalopathies, it would be interesting to see if this parameter is normal in IGE populations who usually have normal range intelligence.

Combined PSG- and sleep questionnaire-based studies mainly applied the PSQI and ESS. In six of eight studies reporting PSQI and polysomnographic parameters, self-assessed sleep quality was lower in patients than controls [31 e 33,37,38,40 e 42]. Interestingly, four of those six articles also showed signi /uniFB01 cantly lower sleep ef/uniFB01 ciency in patients [33,37,41,42]. Chronotype assessment revealed con /uniFB02 icting results. Even though subjects were most commonly intermediate types on MEQ, one study each was in favor of proportionally more morning [44], intermediate [48] or evening types [45] among patients compared to healthy controls. MCQ showed a tendency towards earlier sleep schedules in patients [44,50]. Earlier evidence suggested predominantly evening types in JME patients [68,69]. As circadian rhythm develops towards earlier sleeping and wake-up times with advancing age [70], interpretation of included studies remains dif /uniFB01 cult due to wide age range of subjects. Interestingly, the normal increase of morning types in older IGE patients was not observed by Kendis et al., 2015 [45]. While two studies reported signi /uniFB01 cantly more sleep disorders in patients than controls, a third study found no difference between groups [35,47,50]. Gogou et al., 2016 [30] support a higher prevalence of sleepdisordered breathing by more frequent PSG-based diagnosis of obstructive sleep apnea syndrome in IGE patients.

Subjects in included studies carried either mixed IGE or JME diagnoses, so further investigation of differences between the remaining IGE syndromes was not possible.

In this review, VPA, considered /uniFB01 rst-line therapy in IGE patients [71], was the most commonly used ASM. According to a prior systematic review investigating the in /uniFB02 uence of ASM on objective sleep examinations, visually scored sleep recordings in healthy adults on VPA revealed no signi /uniFB01 cant difference compared to controls, whereas results of studies including patients with epilepsy and VPA therapy showed inconsistent results [72].

Three included PSG-based studies compared exclusively VPAtreated to drug-naïve JME patients with con /uniFB02 icting results. While one study found higher %REM and longer CAP cycles in VPA treated patients [38] and another article showed an improved sleep ef /uniFB01 -ciency, increased %N2 and decreased REM arousal index in the VPA group [35], a third study reported PSG parameters and, surprisingly, time spent in epileptic discharges not signi /uniFB01 cantly differing between both groups [40]. Information about nocturnal activation of epileptic potentials is of interest because of the reciprocal relationship between sleep quality in /uniFB02 uencing the seizure threshold and epileptic discharges potentially causing sleep fragmentation [9,10]. Another study providing epileptic discharge indices showed higher values for drug-naïve compared to drug-treated patients with a predominance in N1 and N2 stage in both groups [41]. Two included sleep questionnaire-based studies reported comparable PSQI and ESS scores in VPA-treated and drug-naïve JME patients [40,49]. In contrast, a longitudinal study of JME patients revealed a signi /uniFB01 cant reduction of PSQI and consistent ESS scores after initiation of VPA therapy [73]. Because other ASM were rarely used in included studies, we could not assess their in /uniFB02 uence on sleep.

## 4.2. Strength and limitations

The overall low heterogeneity of our meta-analyses might result from the application of strict inclusion criteria focusing on IGE patients compared to exclusively healthy controls. Furthermore, we prevented an overrepresentation of likely overlapping study populations by choosing one preferred study from research group 1 and 2. One major limitation is the usage of different sleep classi /uniFB01 cation systems in included studies, which leads to different scoring of sleep parameters [74]. We possibly overestimated reduced sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency in the IGE group due to some studies reporting a significantly longer time in bed in patients. The clinical relevance of differences in sleep macrostructure between IGE patients and healthy controls must be discussed critically as most parameters of both groups were within a normal range.

## 5. Conclusion

This systematic review and meta-analysis revealed reduced sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency, total sleep time, proportion of N2 stage and prolonged REM onset latency in IGE patients compared to healthy controls. In the majority of reporting studies, sleep quality measured by the PSQI was lower in IGE patients. Considering the close connection between sleep and cognitive functioning, altered sleep architecture and impaired sleep quality may contribute to neuropsychological de /uniFB01 cits in IGE patients. Additionally, sleep quality may be related to behavioral health and overall quality of life. Therefore, we recommend screening for sleep disturbances as an important component in the care of IGE patients.

## Practice points

- 1) Sleep ef /uniFB01 ciency is reduced and subjective sleep quality is lower in people with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy compared to healthy controls.
- 2) While most studies focus on macrostructural sleep parameters, sleep microstructure has been little studied until now.
- 3) Unlike earlier evidence for characteristic evening orientation in Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy, chronotype assessment of included studies showed heterogenous results among people with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy.
- 4) Neuropsychological de /uniFB01 cits are common in people with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy. Considering the important role of sleep in learning and memory processes, patients with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy should be screened for sleep disturbances and individually supported according to their needs in order to improve their psychosocial outcome and quality of life.

## Research agenda

- 1) Future research should investigate differences in quality of sleep and sleep architecture between the various Idiopathic generalized epilepsy syndromes.
- 2) Future studies should explore the interrelationship between sleep and cognitive performance in people with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy.
- 3) Given the potentially higher sensitivity in measuring sleep quality, further evaluation of altered sleep microstructure in patients with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy is required, including CAP parameters in the single sleep stages and slow wave slope.
- 4) Longitudinal studies are needed to understand the in /uniFB02 uence of anti-seizure medication on sleep in people with Idiopathic generalized epilepsy.
