### ABSTRACT

Systematic reviews and meta-analyses are increasingly common in sleep research, although the methodological quality level has been a matter of concern. Efforts towards methodological standardization are needed to ensure the reliability of sleep-related systematic reviews. The development of search strategies is a critical step in a systematic review, which often lead to methodological biases. Standardized search filters have been used to facilitate the development of search strategies. However, such filters have not been developed for sleep medicine. The current study aimed at developing a list of PubMed search filters related to sleep medicine, including specific search strategies for different sleep disorders and sleep conditions. First, a list of sleep disorders and conditions was created for which search filters would be developed. This included most conditions listed in the International Classification of Sleep Disorders - 3rd edition. Additional search filters were developed for proposed disorders not recognized as independent clinical entities, and for other sleep-related conditions. All search strategies were designed specifically for PubMed, by combining relevant MeSH terms and free terms. Nine fully independent and unrelated MeSH terms related to sleep were identified. In total, 91 search filters were developed, related to 71 different sleep-related conditions. With the current work, we aimed to provide a list of reliable search filters organized to cover the field in a broad manner, therefore being useful for different types of systematic reviews within sleep medicine, ranging from narrow-focused meta-analyses to broader scoping reviews, mapping reviews, and meta-epidemiological studies.

### 1. Introduction

Systematic reviews with meta-analyses are usually considered the highest level of medical evidence and are becoming increasingly popular as a robust and reliable tool for evidence synthesis and evidence-based research [1–3]. However, a common criticism is that we are facing a mass publication of systematic reviews in health sciences, as the amount of these studies have been growing over recent years, and about 80 of them are published each day. As a consequence, the quality of systematic reviews and meta-analyses has decreased, leading to an increasing number of flawed, misleading or biased studies.

This imbalance between quantity and quality has been observed in sleep medicine, as the publication of systematic reviews and meta-analyses grows steadily in this field. About 230 meta-analyses related to sleep medicine have been published in 2019 (1.1 % of the total publication output) and this number is likely to continue increasing in the coming years. Conversely, the overall methodological validity of meta-analyses in the field of sleep medicine is often poor. Thus, efforts towards methodological standardization are needed to ensure the quality and reliability of systematic reviews related to sleep.

One of the most critical aspects of systematic review methodology is the development of search strategies. Simple literature searches (including unstructured database searching, searching on key journals, and screening reference lists of key articles – i.e. "snowballing") are usually not sufficient, as they might lead to publication bias and to a reduced search accuracy. Conversely, comprehensive and structured search strategies are needed, which represent a critical step in a systematic review, as this determines which records will be further reviewed and analyzed. The proper development of a search strategy is a time-consuming and complex task, encompassing an iterative process of checking and selecting search terms, testing them, and refining the search strategy until a good balance between sensitivity and specificity on the resulting search records is reached. Therefore, developing an adequate search strategy requires clinical/professional experience (to assure a good selection of search words), knowledge of search strategy logic, and familiarity with the search mechanisms and syntax of a given database. Although guidelines and supporting information about how to properly build search strategies are available, the overall quality of search strategies in systematic reviews is rather low [14–16]. It is estimated that at least 82.5 % of the search strategies contain at least one important error that can reduce the search accuracy.

An alternative approach to circumvent these problems is to rely on previously available and standardized search filters, which are developed to accurately identify and retrieve the relevant literature in a given research field. These search strategies are useful for authors of systematic reviews so that they can avoid errors while performing these tasks themselves, while also assuring a better time management. Search filters like these have been proposed for several research fields (e.g. preclinical/animal research, primate research, anesthesiology, limb prostheses, periodontitis, and adverse drug effects), as well as for different research designs (e.g., diagnostic accuracy studies, prognosis studies, and systematic reviews).

To our knowledge, no specific standardized search filter or strategy has been proposed in the field of sleep medicine. The current study aims to develop a list of PubMed search filters related to sleep medicine, including specific search strategies for different sleep disorders and sleep conditions. It provides a "dictionary" of search filters that might be used by sleep researchers, both to identify the relevant literature and as a tool for the performance of systematic reviews in the field.

### 2. Methods

#### 2.1. List of search strategies

The first step relates to developing a list of sleep disorders, conditions and related topics for which search filters would be developed. The first and most comprehensive search strategy is simply related to sleep in the broadest sense. It is intended to be used for broader systematic reviews covering a broader field of study, identifying gaps in existing research, or assessing the quality and patterns in the published literature is particularly relevant, as in cases of scoping reviews, mapping reviews, and meta-epidemiological studies. Next, the list of search strategies followed the sleep disorders contained in the International Classification of Sleep Disorders – 3rd edition – Text Revision – (ICSD-3-TR). Most sleep disorders, isolated symptoms, and normal variants in this source were included, regardless of their clinical relevance or prevalence. Exceptions are related to those diagnoses due to other medical disorders or due to medication or substance, which were not included.

Additional search filters were developed for emergent or proposed disorders that are not listed in ICSD-3-TR as an independent clinical entity, such as sexsomnia, upper airway resistance syndrome and restless sleep disorder. A few additional search strategies not related to the items featured in ICSD-3-TR were also added (including sleep deprivation and dreaming).

Based on the search strategies for each individual sleep disorder, higher-level search strategies were developed for groups of disorders whenever they could be grouped into a concept. This includes each category of disorders listed in the ICSD-3-TR (insomnia, sleep-related breathing disorders, central disorders of hypersomnolence, circadian rhythm sleep-wake disorders, sleep-related movement disorders and parasomnias), as well as for most subcategories. This was usually done by simply merging all search strategies of individual disorders within a category.

In some cases, alternative search strategies within a sleep disorder or condition were developed. This intends to: 1. Provide different levels of sensitivity and specificity (such as for bruxism, sleep eating syndrome and sleep-related hypoxemia disorders). 2. Provide search options for either broader or specific diagnostic precision (such as for narcolepsy, in which there are strategies for the disease in general, or specific for narcolepsy type 1 or type 2). 3. To provide different perspectives in a context related to the specific sleep disorders (such as for sleep-related movement disorders, for which there are strategies either including or excluding bruxism). 4. To include entities that might be of more restricted interest (such as for sleep deprivation, in which there is a search strategy including words related to sleep deprivation models used in preclinical research). Each search strategy is followed by notes explaining or detailing it further. #### 2.2. Search strategies development

All search strategies were designed specifically for a PubMed search syntax, as this is recommended over a direct Medline search. We preferred not to use search syntaxes compatible with other search platforms (such as OVID). While PubMed searching is freely available worldwide with no restrictions, other search platforms might be of limited availability. Using these search strategies in other databases would require adjustment to their specific searching syntaxes and rules. A simple version of the search strategies free of PubMed-related features is available as supplementary material and can be used for adaptation to other databases (Table S1).

Each search strategy was composed by combining controlled vocabulary terms (or thesaurus) and free terms. PubMed/Medline controlled vocabulary are named MeSH (Medical Subject Headings) and the relevant MeSH terms were searched directly on the MeSH database (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/mesh). MeSH terms related to drugs or neurotransmitters were not considered, as using such terms could lead to selection bias. Likewise, terms classified as "supplementary concept" or "pharmacological action" were disregarded. More than one MeSH term could be used in the same search strategy, and whenever applicable, the hierarchically highest relevant term within a branch was chosen.

Free terms (or text words) were chosen based on multiple sources, including the disorder name, alternative nomenclature (as disclosed by the ICSD-3-TR), the entry terms listed in MeSH and clinical experience. Some free search terms might include unusual or old nomenclature, especially when based on the list of entry terms from MeSH (e.g.: "sleep-onset association disorder", as a synonym for insomnia, or "Gelineau syndrome", for narcolepsy), but they were kept for the sake of completeness. The search strategies were built to encompass variations in plural forms, variation between British and American spelling (e.g.: "apnoea" or "apnea"), writing variations (e.g.: "jet lag" or "jetlag"), and synonyms (e.g.: "sleepwalking" or "somnambulism"). Truncation (use of asterisk), use of parenthesis, use of quotation marks, search tags and other searching features were used to refine search strategies and increase sensitivity. Most free terms were used along with a search tag for title, abstracts and keywords (i.e. [tiab]), and free terms alone were avoided (i.e. free term not followed by any search features or tags). Although this deactivates the use of PubMed's automatic term mapping, it increases the search sensitivity, as some searches by free terms alone might bring spurious search results. As an example, a single search by "sleep" with no other search functionality or search tags might retrieve studies authored by individuals named "Sleep" or affiliated to places such as "Division of Pulmonary and Sleep Medicine", even if the article has no direct relationship to sleep medicine whatsoever. Table 2 explains and exemplifies how some search functions were used in the development of these strategies.

The use of acronyms in search strategies could be problematic as they might reduce search specificity. They were used only in cases where they are very specific, widely used, and not easily confused with acronyms from other areas. When included, they were combined with some other term to assure specificity (e.g.: [OSA AND sleep]). Medical tests are only included in the general sleep search, but not in the search strategies for each disorder, as this would lead to reduced search accuracy. Conversely, indexes or diagnostic measures are included whenever they are sufficiently specific (such as AHI and RERA for sleep-disordered breathing). Symptoms or pathophysiological characteristics were not usually added into the search strategies unless they are completely specific.

The development of search strategies is an iterative process and at first, they are refined based on authors' experience and constant testing in PubMed. The search strings presented here are the results of multiple and iterative testing rounds in each case.

### 3. Results

Nine fully independent and unrelated MeSH terms could be identified, implying that they are not hierarchically related, although they could come from the same MeSH branch. Also, a single MeSH term could appear in more than one branch. The MeSH terms identified are disclosed in Table 3. The MeSH terms were used in the search strategies without modification, except for "Chronobiology Disorders". This MeSH term includes a surrogate term for the Smith-Magenis syndrome, a condition that is not primarily related to the concept of circadian rhythm sleep-wake disorders. Therefore, all uses of this MeSH term were corrected as: `("Chronobiology Disorders"[mh] NOT "Smith-Magenis Syndrome"[mh])`.

From each MeSH term, we obtained the total number of retrieved records and the number of exclusive records (i.e. the number of records retrieved by only one MeSH term but not by the others). The three MeSH terms with the higher number of retrieved records are "Sleep Wake Disorders"[mh] (103,215 records) and Sleep[mh] (92,155 records). Considering a combination of all MeSH terms, which retrieved 166,933 records, a simple search for "Sleep Wake Disorders"[mh] retrieved 61.8 % of it, while Sleep[mh] retrieves 55.2 % of it. Also, both of these MeSH terms are highly specific, as the number of exclusive retrieved records was high (54.0 % for "Sleep Wake Disorders"[mh] and 60.5 % for Sleep[mh]).

The third MeSH term with more search results is Polysomnography[mh], with 24,094 retrieved records (14.4 %). Curiously, this MeSH term is highly unspecific, contributing only 951 exclusive records (3.9% of its total search results). This means that adding this search term is not a determinant for a general search about sleep, as most of the articles the term brings would be already brought by searching for other MeSH terms. The opposite is seen with "Nocturnal enuresis"[mh], which retrieves less records (1,369), but 90.1% of these are exclusive. Finally, the MeSH term "Sleep Phase Chronotherapy"[mh] proved to be underutilized in PubMed, as it retrieved only 18 studies, with only 6 of them being exclusive. An overview of the number and percentage of total and exclusive records retrieved is available in Table 3.

In total, 71 sleep-related conditions were identified for the development of search strategies, being mostly sleep disorders, but also isolated symptoms, normal variants, and others. A total of 91 search strategies were developed (considering that more than one search strategy was developed for some sleep disorders (Table 1)). They are organized into nine sections, the first being related to sleep in general, followed by sections devoted to each of the sleep disorders categories presented in the ICSD-3-TR (insomnia, sleep-related breathing disorders, central disorders of hypersomnolence, circadian rhythm sleep-wake disorders, sleep-related movement disorders, parasomnias, and sleep-related medical and neurological disorders). The final section encompasses search strategies covering other sleep-related conditions. Importantly, as this is an iterative and collaborative work, small and periodic changes and refinements to the search strategies might be made. Also, any reader is invited to submit their inputs and suggestions for either updates on the search strategies or new search strategies to be developed. Therefore, the material available as a supplementary material should be understood as a 1.0 version of this search strategy list. Updates will be regularly posted at the Open Science Framework (OSF available at https://osf.io/tdqyp/). Readers are strongly advised to look out for updated versions of this tool at OSF, rather than relying on the current paper as a final source of information, to assure using the most updated version of the search strategies.

### 5. Discussion

Reproducibility and good practices in the performance of systematic reviews and meta-analyses have been a matter of debate in medical sciences in general and this discussion has recently reached sleep medicine. The average methodological quality of systematic review and meta-analysis in sleep medicine is low, as observed in many other fields.

Developing reliable and robust search strategies are among the most challenging steps in systematic reviews, therefore leading to many methodological biases and contributing significantly to the overall reduced methodological quality. Due to these challenges, some strategies have been used to facilitate the process of developing and applying search strategies for systematic reviews, including the use of automating tools and standardized search strategies [17–25], but to date none of them have been directed to sleep medicine.

With the current work, we aimed to provide a list of reliable search filters and strings for multiple sleep-related disorders and conditions. These search strategies were organized to cover the field in a broad manner, therefore being useful for different types of systematic reviews within sleep medicine.

This list of search strategies should not be understood as a definitive work, and adaptations and revisions may be needed over time. The search strategies will be continuously updated and a record of edits and changes on them all will be available on OSF (https://osf.io/tdqyp/).

In conclusion, by means of the present manuscript we intended to provide a reliable source of search strings in sleep medicine. This might be a valuable supporting material for any researcher intending to develop systematic reviews on sleep medicine, saving time and resources from researchers. With that, we hope to work towards the standardization and reporting of systematic reviews in sleep medicine.
