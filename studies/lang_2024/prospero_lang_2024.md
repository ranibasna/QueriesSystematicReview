## REVIEW TITLE AND BASIC DETAILS

## Review title

Sex differences in the association between short sleep duration and the incidence of obesity in adults: a systematic review and meta-analysis

## Review objectives

This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to evaluate and pool the evidence from prospective studies of the impact that sex differences have on the association between short sleep duration and the incidence of obesity in the general adult population.

A second aim will be to test whether there are any interactions between age, study quality and sleep assessment method and sex with respect to their effect on the association between sleep duration and obesity.

## Keywords

Meta-analysis, Obesity, Overweight, Sex, Sleep duration, Systematic review

## SEARCHING AND SCREENING

## Searches

The following electronic bibliographic databases will be searched: MEDLINE, EMBASE, Web of Science.

The search will be restricted to the English language. There will be no restriction on the publication period, all studies will be included from the inception up to January 2023.

Articles will also be identified through citation tracking, by reviewing reference lists of key articles to ensure all relevant studies are included.

Unpublished studies will not be included.

Additional search strategy information can be found in the attached PDF document (link provided below).

## Study design

Inclusion: Original published article, prospective cohort study design with a minimum of 1 year of observation, studies published in English

Exclusion: Case-control studies, Cross-sectional studies, Case series, reviews, comments, letters.

## ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA

## Condition or domain being studied

Obesity, defined as a body mass index of ≥30 kg/m2, afflicts 650 million adults worldwide. However, this simple classification fails to capture the wide variability in disease phenotype. A key moderator of this phenotype is sex. Women have a higher worldwide prevalence of obesity, they have higher body fat content and a gynoid fat distribution as opposed to an android distribution, as well as having a distinct association with metabolic diseases (Palmer and Clegg, 2015, Yang et al., 2021). At present, obese men and women are diagnosed and managed the same way.

Obesity aetiology encompasses genetic, socioeconomic and lifestyle factors. Systematic reviews identified an association between short sleep duration and obesity incidence (Cappuccio et al., 2008, Zhou et al., 2019). It is unclear whether this association varies with sex. In the general population, adult women report more disrupted sleep and a longer total sleep time than men (p&lt;0.001)(Mong and Cusmano, 2016, Kocevska et al., 2021, Jonasdottir et al., 2021). Sex steroids are particularly implicated in sleep physiology with variations seen in women during menopause, menstruation and pregnancy (Mong and Cusmano, 2016). It is, therefore, physiologically plausible that the association between short sleep duration and obesity varies between men and women.

## Population

Inclusion: Studies performed in the general population, adults (aged 18 years or older at baseline)

Exclusion: Children and adolescents (aged 0-17years at baseline), studies performed in patient populations, studies performed in populations with a pre-diagnosed sleep disorder like obstructive sleep apnoea, studies performed in shift-work populations.

## Intervention(s) or exposure(s)

Inclusion: studies that measured the exposure sleep duration/quantity at baseline (self-reported or objectively assessed using sleep actigraphy)

Exclusion: studies where sleep duration was not measured, studies where the exposure was napping or shift work.

## Comparator(s) or control(s)

This review will compare the outcome for adult men and women. Inclusion: Studies that provide separate outcome measures for men and women in the data.

Studies where data in men and women can be extracted and compared separately. Exclusion: Studies that do not separate men and women in the data.

## Context

No restrictions will be placed on the adult population in terms of sample size, nationality, the country where the research was conducted, and socioeconomic status.

In the case of multiple publications being identified for the same study, the study with the longest follow-up and most detailed methodology will be included.

## OUTCOMES TO BE ANALYSED

## Main outcomes

Primary outcome: incidence of new obesity as measured by BMI ≥30kg/m2 (unless stated otherwise in the individual study), changes in BMI, BMI z-scores.

## Measures of effect

Rate ratio between women and men for incidence

Mean difference for changes in BMI and BMI z-scores. Kilograms, BMI units.

## DATA COLLECTION PROCESS

## Data extraction (selection and coding)

Study selection:

Two reviewers (ML, CN) will independently screen records, apply the eligibility criteria and select studies for inclusion in the systematic review.

The reviewers will not be blinded to each other's decisions and any discrepancies that arise will be discussed. Differences in study selection will be resolved by discussion and consensus with a third investigator.

Software system Rayyan (or similar) will be used to facilitate study selection.

Extracted data will include:

- • First author
- • Year of publication
- • Country of origin of the population
- • Recruitment year of cohort
- • Age at sleep assessment
- • Sex of population
- • Duration of follow-up
- • The number of participants included
- • Methods of assessment of exposure (sleep duration)
- • Definitions of sleep categories
- • Number of people in each sleep category
- • Definitions of outcomes
- • Reported changes in outcome (BMI, weight, BMI z-score)
- · Findings expressed as incidence rate ratios, odds ratios or relative risks. Including 95% confidence intervals and covariates adjusted for

Data will be recorded in an excel spreadsheet. ML will independently extract data and CN will check the extracted data. Differences in extracted information will be resolved by discussion and consensus with a third investigator.

When data is not readily available from the published reports but the authors indicated that this data was measured, lead authors will be contacted to obtain findings of analysis stratified by sex.

## Risk of bias (quality) assessment

The validated scoring tool, the Downs and Black Quality Index will be used to assess the quality of the individual studies included in the systematic review.

## PLANNED DATA SYNTHESIS

## Strategy for data synthesis

We will systematically describe the study characteristics in the narrative part of the review.

The main outcome will be a comparison of the odds ratios between men and women for developing obesity in those who have a short duration of sleep compared with those who have a longer duration of sleep. Numerically the outcome can be estimated as a ratio of odds ratios. Confidence intervals will be calculated for individual studies.

The heterogeneity among studies will be tested using the Q-statistic and the contribution of between-study variability relative to total variability will be estimated by I² statistic. Funnel plots will be assessed for evidence of small study effects, if appropriate Egger's test will be utilised to judge funnel plot symmetry.

A sensitivity analysis will be performed to assess the effects of an individual study on the overall meta-analysis, this will be undertaken by omitting one study at a time.

Softwares dedicated to meta-analysis will be utilised such as STATA or RevMan.

## Analysis of subgroups or subsets

We plan to explore the influence of age, study quality and sleep assessment methods on the effect of sex on the association between sleep duration and obesity by examining statistical interactions between sex and these variables.

## Revision note 1 change

At the stage of piloting the study selection process, the inclusion criteria were amended to include longitudinal cohort studies which could be either retrospective or prospective. The original protocol included only prospective studies however, several studies which were retrospective were identified during the piloting stage to meet the inclusion criteria. For these studies the observation period was &gt;12months and the outcome was not present in participants at the start of the observation period.
