## Noise as a sleep aid: a systematic review

Michael Smith, Mathias Basner, Sarah Rocha, Samantha Riedy

## Citation

Michael Smith, Mathias Basner, Sarah Rocha, Samantha Riedy. Noise as a sleep aid: a systematic review. PROSPERO 2024 Available from https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/PROSPERO/view/CRD42020148736

## REVIEW TITLE AND BASIC DETAILS

## Review title

Noise as a sleep aid: a systematic review

## Review objectives

Does the use of so-called "white noise machines" improve sleep latency, sleep continuity, sleep quality and/or decrease arousals, awakenings and wakefulness?

## Keywords

Masking noise, Sleep, White noise

## SEARCHING AND SCREENING

## Searches

Sources searched: PubMed, Web of Science, and Scopus.

Search dates: February 2019, to be repeated prior to the final analysis.

Restrictions: English language studies only, journal articles and conference proceedings only.

## Study design

There will be no restrictions on the types of study eligible for inclusion.

## ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA

## Condition or domain being studied

Commercially-available white noise machines (smartphone apps and standalone devices) are growing in popularity. However, the scientific evidence for their efficacy in promoting sleep and improving sleep quality is lacking.

## Population

Human subjects only; can be neonates, infants, children and/or adults.

No animal or fetal studies to be included.

## Intervention(s) or exposure(s)

Continuous broadband acoustic noise played during sleep and sleep onset, including white noise, pink noise, broadband noise, and natural sounds that are generally broadband in nature (including but not limited to ocean waves, rainfall, waterfalls, flowing water, wind blowing through leaves).

Not included are bursts of noise used either disturb sleep or to enhance slow wave sleep. These interventions can be implemented in any setting, including hospitals, the home, during travel, and in laboratory studies.

## Comparator(s) or control(s)

Control situations will be sleep situations without the noise intervention, and can include withinsubject cross-over studies, cross-sectional studies, longitudinal studies, and randomized control studies with the intervention measured in an intervention and a control group.

## Context

The interventions can be implemented in any setting, including hospitals, the home, during travel, and in laboratory studies.

## OUTCOMES TO BE ANALYSED

## Main outcomes

Sleep onset latency, defined as the time from lights out to the start of sleep.

Total sleep time and sleep efficiency, defined as the total time spent asleep, and the percentage of time in bed spent asleep, respectively.

Wakefulness after sleep onset (WASO), defined as the total time spent awake between sleep onset and the final rise time.

Sleep fragmentation, defined as the absolute amount and relative frequency of arousals and awakenings.

Sleep architecture, defined as the relative duration and timings of different sleep stages.

Sleep quality, subjective wellbeing and/or performance, defined using self-reported questionnaires.

## Measures of effect

Effects will be measured during the sleep period of interest if using objective measures, immediately following the sleep period if using self-reported measures, and may include retrospective studies asking participants to recall the effects over a specified time period.

## Additional outcomes

None.

## Measures of effect

Not applicable.

## DATA COLLECTION PROCESS

## Data extraction (selection and coding)

Two reviewers will independently and blindly judge the article abstracts retrieved during the searches against the eligibility criteria for potential inclusion. For records for which the decision regarding eligibility is not unanimous, a third reviewer will independently judge whether the record should be included. Also, if it is unclear from the abstract alone whether a record should be included or excluded, then the full text will be reviewed before a decision is made.

Data will then be extracted from the studies selected for inclusion.

One researcher will initially extract the following data: study purpose, sleep measurement methodology, study setting, type of noise exposure, noise level, noise duration, location of noise source, number of study nights, number of participants, age, sex, study inclusion/exclusion criteria, statistical analyses employed, and objective and subjective sleep measures. The extracted data will be recorded in an MS Excel spreadsheet.

A second researcher will check the extracted data against the original records prior to data analysis, and any disagreements in judgments will be resolved by consensus involving a third researcher.

## Risk of bias (quality) assessment

For each outcome variable of interest, we will assess the quality of evidence according to the GRADE criteria.

At the study level, we will consider methods of randomization, appropriateness of statistical analysis, acoustical characteristics of the noise intervention, use of control conditions, study population compliance with the intervention, and whether concurrent interventions were introduced.

At the outcome level, we will consider the measures of the sleep outcome (e.g. objective polysomnography vs. actigraphy vs. self-report vs. behavioral observation), heterogeneity or homogeneity of the study population, width of confidence intervals (if available), number and consistency of studies reporting an effect, and evidence for publication bias across studies.

## PLANNED DATA SYNTHESIS

## Strategy for data synthesis

For each outcome, we will compare consistency across studies for the direction of effect (e.g. shorter sleep onset latency; higher total sleep time and sleep efficiency, decreased WASO and sleep fragmentation, increased slow wave sleep, higher sleep quality).

We anticipate small effect sizes that may vary depending on study methodology, and will therefore assess consistency in the direction of effect across studies, with consistent findings considered as providing a basis of supporting evidence, whereas inconsistent findings will be considered as indicating uncertainty in the efficacy of the white noise intervention.

We do not anticipate there will be sufficient data to perform a quantitative meta-analysis. Data synthesis will therefore be narrative, with the main outcome being an assessment of the strength of evidence for each outcome examined, according to the GRADE criteria.

Evidence from studies using objective measures of sleep (polysomnography and actigraphy, which are valid and sensitive measures of sleep-wake behaviors.), large sample sizes, and reliable statistical analysis will hold greater weight. Studies relying on external observation to assess sleep, poor statistical analysis, confounding mediators such as concurrent interventions (e.g. eye masks), poor study compliance (e.g. participants only intermittently using the white noise machine) and lack of study control groups will be considered as holding less weight.

For each outcome, we will consider a narrative synthesis of the results only if a minimum of five relevant studies have been published. For outcomes where there are too few papers to assess the quality of evidence using the GRADE criteria, or results are inconsistent across studies, we will briefly describe the results without making a recommendation. For each outcome we will assess the quality of evidence according to the GRADE criteria, as either high, moderate, low or very low.
