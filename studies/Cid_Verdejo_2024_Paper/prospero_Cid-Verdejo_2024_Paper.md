## Context 1 change

Bruxism, a condition characterized by repetitive jaw-muscle activity involving teeth clenching or grinding, and/or bracing or thrusting of the mandible, can manifest in two distinct ways - sleep bruxism (SB) or awake bruxism (AB). These two types may have different causes and potential consequences. Traditionally, clinical diagnosis of SB relies on self- or partner-reported history of tooth grinding and observation of clinical signs and symptoms. However, these findings do not always linearly correlate with SB activity. Therefore, polysomnography (PSG) with audio-video (AV) recordings has emerged as the reference standard for definitive SB diagnosis, despite its disadvantages such as high cost, potential need for multiple nights, laboratory setting for AV recordings, examiner's skill, and patient compliance.The validity of these alternative methods in comparison with PSG has been tested separately in previous studies. A recent systematic review has provided an overview of the validity of portable instrumental devices for measuring SB compared to PSG, but a comprehensive appraisal of the validity of all available methods is lacking. ## DATA COLLECTION PROCESS

## Data extraction (selection and coding)

During phase 1 of the study selection process, titles and abstracts were screened, and studies were excluded if they were reviews, letters, personal opinions, book chapters, or conference abstracts. Additionally, studies that did not compare methods of SB diagnosis with PSG, the reference standard, were also excluded. In phase 2, full-text articles were reviewed, and studies were further excluded if they did not perform full overnight PSG, did not present diagnostic validity measurements such as sensitivity and specificity, or did not provide enough data to calculate these measures. Studies that focused on a different target condition or had duplicated samples were also excluded from the analysis. There were no restrictions based on age, sex, time of publication, or language in the study selection process.

## PLANNED DATA SYNTHESIS

## Strategy for data synthesis 1 change

The Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS) will be used to assess the methodological quality of the included studies, as recommended by other authors for cohort studies [23]. The NOS evaluates the quality of studies based on three domains: selection (4 items), comparability (1 item), and outcomes (3 items) [24]. The "selection" and "outcome" domain scores range from 0 to 1, while the "comparability" domain score ranges from 0 to 2, with a total score ranging from 0 to 9, where higher scores indicate better quality. The studies will be grouped into three categories based on their NOS scores, as previous studies have done: good quality (&gt;7/9 points), fair quality (&gt;5-7/9 points), and low quality (0-4/9 points).Three independent reviewers (XXXXX) will assess the risk of bias in the included studies. Additionally, the kappa coefficient (κ) and the percentage of agreement scores will be calculated to assess the reliability of the reviewers' assessments before reaching any consensus. Inter-rater reliability will be estimated using κ&gt;0.7, indicating a high level of agreement between the reviewers; κ of 0.5-0.7, indicating a moderate level of agreement; and κ&lt;0.5, indicating a low level of agreement. This approach will ensure that the assessment of risk of bias in the included studies is reliable and consistent.
